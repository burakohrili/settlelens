## How Alimony Is Calculated in 2026

Alimony — also called spousal support or spousal maintenance — is one of the most financially consequential decisions in any divorce. Yet most people have no idea how the number is calculated until they're sitting in an attorney's office. Here's what you need to know before that meeting.

---

## There Is No National Alimony Formula

Unlike child support, which follows state-specific guidelines, alimony in most states is left to judicial discretion. Judges weigh a range of factors:

- **Length of the marriage**
- **Each spouse's income and earning capacity**
- **Standard of living during the marriage**
- **Career sacrifices** (one spouse leaving work to raise children, supporting the other's education)
- **Age and health** of each spouse
- **Child custody arrangements**

A few states have moved toward income-based formulas or guidelines, but even those allow judicial adjustment. The result: outcomes vary widely.

---

## The Most Common Calculation Methods

### Method 1: Income Percentage Difference (Widely Used)

A common approach used by courts and mediators:

> **(Payor's monthly net income × 35%) − (Recipient's monthly net income × 25%)**

**Example:**
- Payor earns $4,200/month net
- Recipient earns $1,400/month net
- ($4,200 × 35%) − ($1,400 × 25%) = $1,470 − $350 = **$1,120/month**

This method produces a rough estimate, not a guaranteed outcome. Courts adjust for all the factors listed above.

### Method 2: State-Specific Formulas

Some states have moved toward more formulaic approaches:

| State | Approach |
|---|---|
| **Virginia** | Advisory guidelines (% of income difference × years) |
| **Massachusetts** | Reform legislation — 30–35% of income difference is common |
| **New York** | Statutory formula for temporary support; post-divorce is discretionary |
| **Texas** | Cap on duration; limited to 20% of payor's average monthly gross income |
| **California** | No fixed formula; Santa Clara guideline is informal but widely used |

Even in formula states, the result is a starting point — not a ceiling or floor.

## How Long Does Alimony Last?

Duration depends heavily on marriage length:

| Marriage Length | Typical Alimony Duration |
|---|---|
| Under 5 years | Often 0–2 years (or none) |
| 5–10 years | 2–5 years |
| 10–20 years | 5–10 years |
| Over 20 years | Potentially indefinite |

**Key termination triggers:**
- Recipient remarries → almost always terminates alimony immediately
- Recipient cohabitates with a partner → terminates or reduces in many states
- Payor retires → grounds for modification
- Either party dies

## The 10-Year Dollar Reality

The monthly number rarely gets the attention it deserves. Over time:

| Monthly Amount | 3 Years | 5 Years | 8 Years | 10 Years |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $500 | $18,000 | $30,000 | $48,000 | $60,000 |
| $800 | $28,800 | $48,000 | $76,800 | $96,000 |
| $1,200 | $43,200 | $72,000 | $115,200 | $144,000 |

For the paying spouse, alimony directly reduces savings capacity and retirement contributions for years. For the receiving spouse, it provides an income bridge — but one with an end date that must be planned for.

## The 2019 Tax Law Change — Still Misunderstood

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) eliminated the alimony deduction starting January 1, 2019:

- **Pre-2019 agreements:** Alimony is **deductible** for the payor and **taxable income** for the recipient
- **Post-2018 agreements:** Alimony is **not deductible** and **not taxable** — treated as an after-tax transfer

This changes the negotiating math significantly. A $1,000/month payment is now worth the same after-tax dollars to both parties. Make sure your financial model reflects this.

## How to Negotiate from a Financially Informed Position

Most people negotiate alimony emotionally. A better approach:

1. **Know the range.** What would a judge likely award in your jurisdiction? Research local cases or ask your attorney.
2. **Model the scenarios.** $700/month for 5 years vs. $500/month for 8 years produces different total payments and cash flow profiles.
3. **Consider a lump-sum alternative.** Trading a monthly stream for a one-time payment eliminates duration risk for both parties.
4. **Factor in modification risk.** Either party can petition for modification if income changes substantially.
